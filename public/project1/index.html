Project 1: Exploratory Data Analysis

January 1, 0001   

Introduction

The two datasets that were chosen are a 2016 national county voting data dataset, and a 2016 national county health outcomes data dataset. Both are organized by row, with each row being a county. The voting data contains a variety of statistics, like percentage of a count voting base under 29, percentage of a county living rurally, total voting age population of a county, votes recorded for democratic senate races, votes recorded for 3rd party presidential races, etc. The health outcomes dataset is massive, with more than 160 variables available. This is obviously far more than necessary. The dataset included a subset containing a variety of health outcome factors (quality of life, socioeconomic status, etc.) in both a county ranking format (from 1 to n in each state) and in quartile format (1-4) for each county in the United States. The voting data was sourced from the MIT election lab, and the health outcomes data was from www.countyhealthrankings.org.

I wanted to join these two datasets because I was curious as to how two particular variables from the health outcomes data, socioeconomic status and quality of life, were correlated with the 2016 presidential race results. I’ve always had an interest in politics and elections in general. Many of the frequent narratives surrounding elections, like rural voters voting republican or minority voters voting democrat, are things that I take as fact without ever having seen any data to support them. In particular, I find that socioeconomic status and quality of life are two variables in which I’ve rarely seen any data to either support or refute frequently made claims surrounding these variables in elections. I decided I wanted to explore them myself and find out whether those two factors actually do influence a county’s voting decisions. # Data Cleaning and Joining ### Data Cleaning The datasets are both very large, and each has multiple variables that are of less interest. Before performing any analysis, the data should be cleaned and adjusted to better serve this project.

Joining Datasets

We are now ready to join together our datasets. We are using an inner join. This is for two reasons. The first is that demo_16 does not include Alaska. This results in the dropping of 70 or so rows. The second reason is there are rows in demo_16 that don’t appear in health_16 as well. Doing an inner join ensures only the common rows are kept.

# join the datasets
data <- health_16 %>% inner_join(demo_16, by = c("fips", 
    "state", "county"))

Notably, none of the variables relating to quality of life or SES are included in these summaries. Those variables are rankings and quartiles, not actual raw data. # PAM Clustering Now we will do PAM clustering with our data. This will attempt to split the data into two separate clusters, as determined by distance between observations. This distance is found using Gowers dissimilarity. Clustering works by picking random points as your clusters and dsitributing your data points to two clusters based on thir similarity to the random points. You then calculate a new mean of the points in each group, and repeat the clustering based on distance. This repeats until the clusters stabilize, and each data point remains in the same group. All of this takes place within the pam() function.

The first thing we will do is determine the optimal number of clusters for the data. This is done by finding the highest average silhouette width for a variety of k, or number of clusters. As the graph demonstrates, the highest silhouette width occurs when k = 2, so we will use 2 clusters for our PAM clustering.

pam_data <- data %>% ungroup %>% select(quality_q, ses_q, 
    pct_29, pct_65, pct_rural, pct_rep) %>% filter(ses_q != 
    5) %>% na.omit()

# get number of clusters
sil_width <- vector()
for (i in 2:10) {
    pam_fit <- pam_data %>% daisy("gower") %>% pam(gower1, 
        k = i, diss = T)
    sil_width[i] <- pam_fit$silinfo$avg.width
}
ggplot() + geom_line(aes(x = 2:10, y = sil_width[2:10])) + 
    scale_x_continuous(name = "k", breaks = 2:10) + labs(title = "Figure 4: 2016 national county clusters: average silhouette width vs k", 
    y = "Average silhouette width") + theme_minimal() + 
    theme(plot.title = element_text(hjust = 0.5))

Next, we can visualize the silhouette widths and find the average. This graph presents both groups and their silhouettes widths. The average width is reported as 0.34, which is fairly weak clustering and indicates that any observed clustering could be completely artificial.
